Find Homes for Sale in Miami

Miami is one of the most competitive real estate markets in the country. Demand is consistent, inventory is limited, and the buyer pool is international. Whether you are looking for a high-rise condo in the urban core or a single-family home in an established residential neighborhood, Miami rewards buyers who are prepared and move decisively.

What to Expect in the Miami Market

Miami's real estate market is defined by density and scarcity. The city has limited developable land, and desirable neighborhoods see low turnover. Prices in established areas have appreciated steadily over the past decade, and the post-2020 migration of financial and technology firms to South Florida has accelerated demand at every price point.

Median home prices in Miami vary significantly by submarket. Condo prices in Brickell and Miami Beach range from $400,000 for a studio to $5M+ for luxury units. Single-family homes in Coral Gables, Coconut Grove, and South Miami typically start at $800,000 and climb well above $2M in the most desirable streets.

Miami's Key Neighborhoods for Buyers

Brickell is Miami's financial district and urban residential hub. Glass towers line Brickell Avenue and the bay. The neighborhood is walkable, transit-accessible, and appeals primarily to young professionals and international buyers. School options in the immediate area are limited, making it a stronger fit for buyers without school-age children.

Coral Gables is Miami's most established residential neighborhood. The City Beautiful — as it is formally named — features Mediterranean-Revival architecture, canopied streets, and some of the best public schools in Miami-Dade County. Inventory is scarce. When homes come to market, they move quickly.

Coconut Grove is one of Miami's oldest neighborhoods and one of its most livable. Proximity to Biscayne Bay, a walkable village center, and a quieter pace than Brickell or Miami Beach make it consistently desirable. Single-family inventory is limited; waterfront properties are rare and priced accordingly.

Wynwood and Edgewater attract buyers interested in proximity to the arts district and Biscayne Bay. Both neighborhoods have seen significant appreciation over the past five years and continue to attract new residential development.

Miami Beach and South Beach serve a specific buyer: those who prioritize walkability, beach access, and urban energy. The condo market here is deep and varied. Buyers should conduct thorough due diligence on building financials, reserve funding, and pending assessments.

What Buyers Need to Know

Miami is not a market for unprepared buyers. Competitive listings receive multiple offers. Sellers expect pre-approval letters, reasonable contingency timelines, and buyers who understand the pace of the market.

Florida's condo legislation following the 2021 Surfside collapse has introduced new reserve funding requirements for older buildings. Buyers purchasing in buildings built before 1990 should request reserve studies and financial statements before making an offer.

Flood insurance is a material cost in Miami. Many properties fall within FEMA-designated flood zones. Factor this into your total housing cost calculation before setting a purchase price target.
